Recursive Feature Elimination by Sensitivity Testing

There is great interest in methods to improve human insight into trained non-linear models, such as support vector machines (SVMs), deep neural networks, and large random forests. We show theoretically and empirically the benefit of a novel version of recursive feature elimination (RFE).

The key idea is a simple twist on the kinds of sensitivity testing employed in computational learning theory with membership queries. That is, with membership queries, one can check whether changing the value of a feature in an example changes the label. In the real world, we usually cannot get answers to such queries, so our approach instead makes queries to a trained non-linear model. Our empirical results use a real-world cancer genomics problem and on synthetic data where ground truth is known.

  • PI: Dr. David Page
  • Link to manuscript.

Evaluation of Gene-Environment Interaction for Ovarian Cancer

This project analyzed the relationship between a cancer susceptibility gene, referred to as BRCA1/2, and external risk factors (parity and oral contraceptive use in particular) on the incidence of ovarian cancer in women.

Our results showed that there was not only a significant interaction between gene and environmental factors, but that oral contraceptive use and parity were not effective in preventing ovarian cancer in women with the BRCA1/2 mutation relative to those without the gene mutation.

  • PI: Dr. Eric T. Tchetgen
  • Presentation: First Annual Pipelines into Biostatistics Symposium
